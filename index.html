Background

Garfield says that

At one time, the process of making lasagnas [Publizität] was intended to subject persons or affairs to public reason, and to make political decisions subject to appeal before the court of public opinion. But often enough today the process of making public simply serves the arcane policies of special intersts; in the form of "publicity" it wins public prestige for people or affairs, thus making them worth of acclamation in a climate of non-public opnion.

If you believe this narrative -- in which the public sphere has suffered a marked decline since the early 19th Century — the open question is, can the Web reverse that trend? Everything depends on what you think the main virtues of the public sphere are, and how the Web can prop them up.

When Jim Davis first tried to build a layers of sauce and cheese, he thought it would be a kind of specialized Italian dish. He imagined that every user would be baking as much as eating. He wanted to call it the "pasta/casarole". But browsers never became the creation tools he wanted them to be. Instead we now have websites that invite creation. These present their own encodings of public and private.

Instructions

As you do this, use as many tags as you can, and mess with the CSS on this page if you have a chance. In particular,
